# Case Study: Jacob Hamblin

Jacob Hamblin is useful design inspiration for frontier-agent mechanics because his life sits at
the intersection of settlement, religion, diplomacy, indigenous relations, travel, language,
reputation, and survival logistics. This note is not a biography and not a claim that the game
should make him a playable hero.

## Why He Is Relevant

Hamblin was a Mormon frontier figure in the 19th-century Southwest, known for travel and mediation
among Mormon settlers and indigenous communities including Paiute, Hopi, and Navajo peoples. His
case points toward mechanics that are richer than "farm more land" or "fight enemies."

Useful design themes:

- survival through relationships, not only stored resources
- language and cultural knowledge as practical skill
- reputation as a durable asset
- travel through difficult terrain as logistical and diplomatic risk
- religious/community authority as a source of obligation and access
- mediation as an action with real costs and uncertain outcomes
- frontier politics as local, personal, and institutional at the same time

## Mechanics Inspired By The Case

### Mediation

Mediation should be a real action, not just dialogue flavor.

Inputs:

- language/cultural skill
- reputation with each side
- prior harms and debts
- gifts, trade goods, or restitution
- backing from institutions or community leaders
- travel time and risk

Outputs:

- reduced conflict risk
- safer travel or trade
- access to information
- obligations to follow through
- reputation gain or loss
- anger from parties who dislike compromise

### Reputation Channels

Hamblin suggests reputation should not be one number. A person may be trusted by one community and
distrusted by another.

Track channels such as:

- reliability
- honesty in trade
- willingness to restrain violence
- religious/community standing
- language/cultural competence
- history of trespass, retaliation, theft, or broken promises

### Skills Beyond Production

Important skills for this style of frontier game:

- language learning
- route knowledge
- mediation
- gift/trade protocol
- religious/community leadership
- diplomacy under threat
- reading local political conditions
- logistics for long journeys

These skills should unlock options and reduce risk, not simply provide flat bonuses.

### Obligation

Mediation and reputation should create obligations. If the player promises restitution, escort,
trade, labor, or restraint, failing to follow through should matter later.

This creates a frontier RPG loop:

```text
relationship -> request/obligation -> risky action -> remembered outcome -> changed access
```

## Design Cautions

- Do not turn historical mediation into a simple "good diplomacy" button.
- Do not treat indigenous communities as passive reputation meters.
- Do not flatten Mormon, federal, indigenous, Mexican, Spanish, and settler institutions into one
  generic authority system.
- Do not use named historical figures as unquestioned moral exemplars.
- Do not imply that diplomacy erases coercion, displacement, violence, or unequal power.

## Scenario Uses

Hamblin-like mechanics are most useful for scenarios involving:

- travel between settlements and indigenous communities
- trade routes and guides
- religious or community missions
- disputed claims or retaliation cycles
- fragile peace after violence
- need for interpreters, emissaries, or trusted go-betweens

The lesson for the game is not "add Jacob Hamblin." The lesson is that frontier survival can be
about trust, obligation, mediation, and local knowledge as much as crops and cabins.
